\section{Literature Review}

The literature review presents an overview of existing research related to hospital service robots, medicine delivery systems, and automated pill dispensing mechanisms. It highlights key methodologies, system architectures, and technological integrations that form the foundation for the proposed robotic system.

\subsection{Line Following Robots for Hospital Management}

Autonomous line-following robots have been explored as cost-effective solutions for hospital logistics. Kumaresan \textit{et al.} developed a line-following robot designed to assist hospital operations by transporting medicines, linens, and medical equipment along predefined floor paths \cite{ref1}. The system utilized infrared (IR) sensors for path detection and an Arduino Uno microcontroller for navigation control and decision-making. Additionally, a Bluetooth-based Android interface enabled remote interaction with the robot, enhancing usability and operational flexibility. The study demonstrated that sensor-based mobile robots can effectively reduce staff workload, improve efficiency, and provide scalable automation solutions within clinical environments.

\subsection{Medicine Delivery Robots}

Medicine delivery robots have gained importance, particularly during infectious disease outbreaks requiring minimal human contact. Patel, Sharma, and Randhawa introduced \textit{MedBuddy}, a Bluetooth-controlled robotic system designed to deliver medicines within hospital wards while reducing direct interaction between healthcare workers and patients \cite{ref2}. The system was built on an Arduino Uno platform and incorporated an ultrasonic sensor for obstacle detection and distance measurement. A smartphone camera provided real-time video streaming to a custom mobile application for teleoperation. The research emphasized safe navigation, wireless communication, and contactless medicine distribution as essential features of modern healthcare robotics.

\subsection{Automated Pill Dispensing Systems}

Automation in medication management has also been addressed through intelligent pill dispensing systems. Singh and Rathi implemented a servo-controlled rotating disc mechanism for automated pill dispensing integrated with a Raspberry Pi platform \cite{ref3}. Each pill compartment was synchronized with a real-time clock (RTC) module to ensure time-based medication release. The system further incorporated facial recognition technology to enable personalized medication delivery. Their study highlighted the effectiveness of combining embedded systems and computer vision to improve medication safety, adherence, and reliability in healthcare applications.

\subsection{Robotic System Integration and Control}

Further advancements in robotic system integration and control methodologies are documented in publications from the IOP Conference Series \cite{ref4}. These works commonly address autonomous navigation, sensor integration, control algorithms, and system reliability improvements for mobile robots operating in structured indoor environments. Such research strengthens the technical foundation required for developing reliable autonomous and semi-autonomous robots capable of functioning efficiently in hospital settings, supporting logistics transport and human--robot interaction.
