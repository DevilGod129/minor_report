

The advancement of healthcare robotics has led to the development of autonomous hospital service robots, medicine delivery platforms, and automated pill dispensing systems. This section reviews relevant prior work and identifies the research gaps addressed in the proposed system.

\section{Line-Following Robots for Hospital Management}

A line-following robot for hospital operations was developed in [1]. The system was designed to transport medicines, linens, and medical equipment along predefined floor paths within hospital environments. Infrared (IR) sensors were employed for path detection, while an Arduino Uno microcontroller handled navigation and control tasks. A Bluetooth-based Android interface enabled remote supervision and operational control. The study demonstrated that low-cost embedded systems can significantly improve hospital logistics efficiency and reduce manual workload. However, the proposed system was limited to basic navigation and transportation functions without incorporating intelligent scheduling, patient-specific delivery, or secure authentication mechanisms.

\section{Medicine Delivery Robots}

In [2], a Bluetooth-controlled medicine delivery robot named \textit{MedBuddy} was introduced to minimize direct interaction between healthcare workers and patients, particularly during infectious disease outbreaks. The system utilized an Arduino Uno platform integrated with ultrasonic sensors for obstacle detection and a smartphone camera for real-time video streaming to a mobile application. While the design enhanced contactless medicine distribution and remote monitoring, it relied primarily on manual teleoperation. The absence of autonomous navigation, patient identification, and automated dispensing mechanisms limited its scalability and operational intelligence.

\section{Automated Pill Dispensing Systems}

An automated pill dispensing system integrated with a Raspberry Pi platform was presented in [3]. The system employed a servo-controlled rotating disc mechanism synchronized with a real-time clock (RTC) module to ensure time-based medication release. Furthermore, facial recognition technology was incorporated to enable patient-specific medicine dispensing. Although this approach improved medication adherence and safety, the system was stationary and restricted to bedside usage. It lacked mobility and integration with hospital-wide logistics systems.


Although existing systems demonstrate effective navigation and dispensing capabilities, most lack an integrated framework combining autonomous navigation, patient identification, and intelligent scheduling in a low-cost modular platform. The proposed system aims to address these limitations by incorporating AI-based recognition, modular hardware design, and real-time monitoring features.
